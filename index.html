The "Catalytic Converter" Scam

Dealer said I needed a new cat for $1,400. Threw a P0420 code and they insisted it was failed. Smelled like rotten eggs and power was down. Almost paid it until my buddy suggested checking the downstream O2 sensor first.

The Fix: Replaced the downstream O2 sensor ($89 part, 20 minutes with a socket). Cleared the code, drove 200 miles, no return. The sensor was lazy and throwing false readings. Cat was fine all along.

P0302
2008 Ford F-150 • 98K miles
Saved $800

Shaking Like a Washing Machine

Cylinder 2 misfire on my 5.4L Triton. Shop wanted to replace all 8 coils and plugs for $950. Truck was shaking at idle and throwing P0302 consistently. Compression test showed good numbers, so it wasn't mechanical.

The Fix: Swapped coil #2 with coil #4. Code moved to P0304. Ordered one Motorcraft coil ($65) and replaced the bad one. Also found the COP boot was oil-soaked from a leaking valve cover gasket. Fixed that too with a $12 gasket. Total: $77.

P0135
2005 Toyota Camry • 167K miles
Saved $340

Heater Circuit Drama

Upstream O2 sensor heater circuit code. Shop quoted $450 to "diagnose and replace O2 sensor, possible wiring repair." Car ran fine but wouldn't pass inspection with the CEL on. Code kept coming back immediately after clearing.

The Fix: Checked the O2 sensor fuse first - blown 15A fuse in the engine bay box. Replaced it ($3), code cleared. Fuse blew again a week later. Traced wiring and found the O2 sensor harness was rubbing against the exhaust heat shield. Wrapped with heat tape, secured with zip ties. Fixed for good.

P0455
2015 Jeep Wrangler • 45K miles
Saved $600

Gas Cap Roulette

Large EVAP leak detected. Dealer wanted to smoke test the system ($180) then replace the charcoal canister ($420). Smelled fuel occasionally near the rear of the Jeep. Code came and went randomly, especially after fill-ups.

The Fix: Started with the gas cap - replaced with OEM Mopar cap ($35). Code returned. Checked the EVAP lines near the canister and found the service port cap was missing! Previous mechanic left it off during a fuel pump recall. New cap ($8) and no more codes. Also found a cracked hose clamp.

P0101
2010 Chevy Silverado • 112K miles
Saved $500

Black Death MAF

Mass Air Flow sensor out of range. Truck was running rich, black smoke on acceleration, terrible MPG. Shop said "replace MAF sensor, $380 installed." Had that gut feeling it was simpler.

The Fix: Removed the MAF sensor and it was coated in black oil residue from a K&N filter that was over-oiled. Cleaned it carefully with MAF cleaner spray ($12), let it dry completely. Also found the air filter box wasn't sealing properly - replaced the lid clips ($15). Runs like new, MPG back to 18.

P0171
2009 Subaru Outback • 134K miles
Saved $1,100

The Lean Condition Hunt

System too lean Bank 1. Subaru specialist wanted to replace the front O2 sensor ($280), then the fuel pump ($600), then "possibly injectors." Car was hesitating on cold starts and idling rough when warm.

The Fix: Checked fuel trims with scanner - maxed positive at idle, normalized at 2500 RPM. Classic vacuum leak. Sprayed carb cleaner around intake manifold and found a massive leak at the PCV hose elbow. The rubber was rock hard and cracked. Replaced the PCV valve and hose ($28). Fuel trims back to normal, runs smooth.
